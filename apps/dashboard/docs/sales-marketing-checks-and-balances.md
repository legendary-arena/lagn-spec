# Sales & Marketing Checks & Balances System — Legendary Arena

**Project:** legendary-arena.com / barefootbetters.com
**Last Updated:** June 8, 2026

---

## The Question (Rephrased)

The code checks-and-balances system ensures good software through separation of duties — Architect, Builder, Inspector, Evaluator. But a business isn't just code. The sales funnel, marketing campaigns, customer relationships, and revenue generation all need the same rigor.

Without checks and balances on the business side, common failures include: marketing money spent with no tracking, sales funnels with leaks nobody notices, customers lost because nobody owns retention, and no clear picture of what's working and what's not. These are the business equivalent of shipping untested code.

This document establishes a parallel system for sales and marketing — different roles, different responsibilities, independent oversight — with the owner (Jeff) orchestrating all of it from the dashboard. These principles are foundational and transferable to any business.

## 1. The Four Business Agents

Just as the code system has Architect, Builder, Inspector, and Evaluator — the business side has four parallel roles. Each owns a distinct piece of the sales and marketing machine. No single role handles the full lifecycle of a campaign or customer relationship.

| Business Agent | Role | Code Equivalent | Mandate |
|----------------|------|-----------------|---------|
| **The Strategist** | Planning & Positioning | The Architect | Define WHO we're selling to, WHAT we're selling, and WHY they should care — before any content is created |
| **The Creator** | Content & Campaigns | The Builder | Produce the marketing materials, ads, emails, and content that execute the Strategist's plan |
| **The Analyst** | Metrics & Accountability | The Inspector | Measure what's working, what's not, and where money is being wasted — with data, not opinions |
| **The Auditor** | Business Health & Scalability | The Evaluator | Assess whether the entire business could run without Jeff, be sold, or scale to 10x volume |

**Above all four sits The Orchestrator (Jeff/Owner)** — who doesn't do the work of any single agent but ensures all four are operating, communicating, and aligned toward the same goals.

## 3. Agent 1: The Strategist (Planning & Positioning)

### Code Equivalent: The Architect

### When: BEFORE any content is created or any money is spent

### Responsibilities

The Strategist answers the foundational questions that every marketing dollar depends on. If these are wrong, even brilliant content and perfect execution will fail.

**Who are we selling to?**
- Define the ideal customer profile (ICP) for each product line
  - Online play (core game): Marvel Legendary tabletop fans who want to play online without the setup, teardown, and "who has which cards" friction; deck-building strategy gamers, ages 25–55
  - Premium card sets / expansions: existing Legendary Arena players who've cleared the base content and want new heroes, villains, schemes, and modes
  - Lapsed Legendary players: people who own the physical game but stopped playing — reachable around new-set releases and win-back campaigns
- Each product may have a different ICP — the Strategist defines each one separately

**What are we selling?**
- Not the product — the outcome. The Strategist defines the transformation:
  - "You're not buying access to a card game — you're buying the game-night feeling: sitting across the table from a friend, any night, with no setup."
  - "You're not buying card packs — you're buying the next hour you and a friend will actually look forward to."
- Features vs. benefits: the Strategist ensures all messaging leads with benefits, not specs.

**Why should they care (and why now)?**
- Competitive positioning: what makes Legendary Arena different from playing the physical game or a generic online card game?
  - Answer: faithful online play of the Marvel Legendary deck-builder with zero setup/teardown, the full library of heroes/villains/schemes in one place, deterministic rules enforcement (no rules arguments), and the ability to play any night without getting the group physically together
- Urgency triggers: new card-set releases, seasonal in-game events, holiday gifting season, lapsed-player win-back windows

**Where do we reach them?**
- Channel strategy: which platforms, which communities, which influencers
- Budget allocation: how much per channel, with expected return

**How do we measure success?**
- Define KPIs (Key Performance Indicators) BEFORE the campaign launches
- Every campaign must have a measurable goal: "Convert 50 free players to paid in 30 days" or "Grow email list by 500 subscribers" — not "raise brand awareness" (unmeasurable)

### Deliverable
A **Campaign Brief** — a one-page document that the Creator uses to produce content. Includes: target audience, key message, channels, budget, timeline, and success metrics.

### The Strategist's Rules
1. **"No spend without a brief."** If there's no campaign brief, no money gets spent and no content gets created.
2. **"Lead with the transformation, not the product."** Every message answers: "What changes in the customer's life after they buy this?"
3. **"If you can't measure it, don't do it."** Every campaign has a KPI defined before launch.

### How to Run This Agent with Claude
> "You are the Strategist agent for Legendary Arena. Your job is to create a campaign brief for [this product/launch/initiative]. Define the target audience, the core message (transformation, not features), the channels, the budget, the timeline, and the measurable KPIs. Do NOT create any content — only the strategy. Output a one-page campaign brief."

## 4. Agent 2: The Creator (Content & Campaigns)

### Code Equivalent: The Builder

### When: AFTER the Strategist's campaign brief is approved

### Responsibilities

The Creator produces all customer-facing materials — but ONLY what the campaign brief calls for. No freelancing, no off-brand experiments, no "I thought this would be cool" without the Strategist's sign-off.

**Content types:**
- **Website copy** — product pages, landing pages, homepage messaging
- **Email sequences** — welcome series, abandoned cart recovery, launch announcements, post-purchase follow-up
- **Social media posts** — platform-specific content (YouTube, Instagram, Facebook, TikTok)
- **Ads** — paid search (Google), paid social (Facebook/Instagram), retargeting
- **Video content** — build tutorials, unboxing videos, customer testimonials, YouTube channel content
- **Blog posts / articles** — SEO content targeting relevant search terms
- **Print materials** — the assembly guide, packaging inserts, the origin story card

**For each piece of content, the Creator documents:**
- Which campaign brief it supports
- Which funnel stage it targets (awareness, consideration, decision, retention)
- The call to action (CTA) — what do we want the reader/viewer to DO?
- The A/B variant (if applicable) — always test two versions

### Deliverable
- Completed content assets ready for publishing
- A content log documenting what was created, for which brief, targeting which funnel stage

### The Creator's Rules
1. **"Build to brief."** If the campaign brief says "target lapsed Legendary players on Reddit," don't create a TikTok dance video. Stay on strategy.
2. **"Every piece has a CTA."** No content without a clear next step for the customer (buy, sign up, watch, share).
3. **"No self-publishing."** The Creator doesn't publish or go live without the Analyst confirming tracking is in place. If you can't measure it, you can't learn from it.

### How to Run This Agent with Claude
> "You are the Creator agent for Legendary Arena. Here is the campaign brief. Produce the content assets it calls for — [email sequence / landing page copy / social posts / ad copy]. Follow the brief's target audience, message, and tone exactly. Include a clear CTA in every piece. Do NOT deviate from the brief's strategy."

## 5. Agent 3: The Analyst (Metrics & Accountability)

### Code Equivalent: The Inspector

### When: BEFORE content goes live (tracking setup) and AFTER (performance review)

### Responsibilities

The Analyst is the truth-teller. They don't create content and they don't set strategy — they measure results and report what's actually happening versus what was planned. Without the Analyst, the business runs on gut feelings instead of data.

**Before launch (tracking setup):**
- Confirm analytics tracking is properly installed (Google Analytics, Meta Pixel, email open/click tracking)
- Set up UTM parameters on all links so traffic sources are identifiable
- Confirm conversion tracking is working (can we see when someone actually buys?)
- Set up A/B tests where the Creator has provided variants
- Baseline the current metrics so we can measure the campaign's impact

**After launch (performance review):**
- Track against the KPIs defined in the campaign brief
- Report weekly during active campaigns, monthly for ongoing efforts

**Key metrics the Analyst owns:**

| Funnel Stage | Metric | What It Tells You |
|-------------|--------|-------------------|
| **Awareness** | Website traffic, social impressions, ad reach | Are people seeing us? |
| **Interest** | Email signups, time on site, page views per session | Are people engaging? |
| **Consideration** | Product page views, add-to-cart rate, email click rate | Are people thinking about buying? |
| **Decision** | Conversion rate, cart abandonment rate, checkout completion | Are people buying? |
| **Retention** | Repeat purchase rate, email unsubscribe rate, review count | Are people coming back? |
| **Revenue** | Average order value (AOV), customer lifetime value (LTV), revenue per channel | Is the money math working? |
| **Efficiency** | Customer acquisition cost (CAC), return on ad spend (ROAS), cost per lead | Are we spending wisely? |

**Funnel leak detection:**
The Analyst's most critical job is finding where customers drop off. If 1,000 people visit the product page but only 10 buy, there's a leak. The Analyst identifies exactly where:
- Awareness → Interest: are people bouncing immediately? (messaging mismatch)
- Interest → Consideration: are people reading but not clicking "buy"? (weak CTA or pricing concern)
- Consideration → Decision: are people adding to cart but not checking out? (shipping cost shock, trust issue, complicated checkout)
- Decision → Retention: are people buying once but never returning? (poor post-purchase experience)

Each leak gets a diagnosis and a recommendation — sent back to either the Strategist (messaging problem) or the Creator (content problem).

### Deliverable
- **Weekly campaign report** during active campaigns (traffic, conversions, spend, ROI)
- **Monthly business scorecard** (all KPIs, trends, funnel health)
- **Leak report** whenever a funnel stage shows significant drop-off

### The Analyst's Rules
1. **"Data, not opinions."** Every claim is backed by a number. "I think the ad is working" is not acceptable. "The ad generated 47 clicks at $0.83 each with a 3.2% conversion rate" is.
2. **"No vanity metrics."** Likes, followers, and impressions are not revenue. Report them for context but never as a success metric. The only metric that pays rent is revenue.
3. **"Compare to the brief."** The campaign brief set KPIs. The Analyst reports against those specific KPIs — not whatever metric happens to look good.
4. **"Find the leak."** Every report must identify the weakest point in the funnel and recommend who should fix it (Strategist or Creator).

### How to Run This Agent with Claude
> "You are the Analyst agent for Legendary Arena. Here is the campaign brief with its KPIs, and here is the performance data from [Google Analytics / email platform / ad manager]. Compare actual results to the KPI targets. Identify funnel leaks. Report what's working, what's not, and recommend whether to continue, adjust, or kill this campaign. Use numbers, not opinions."

## 6. Agent 4: The Auditor (Business Health & Scalability)

### Code Equivalent: The Evaluator

### When: QUARTERLY or before any major business decision (scaling, hiring, selling, pivoting)

### Responsibilities

The Auditor steps back from individual campaigns and evaluates the business as a whole. Could this business run without Jeff? Could it be sold? Could it handle 10x the volume? These are the questions that determine long-term viability.

#### A. Revenue Health

- Is revenue growing, flat, or declining month-over-month?
- Is revenue concentrated in one product (risky) or diversified across multiple products (stable)?
- What's the revenue per product line? Which products carry the business?
- What's the gross margin on each product? (Revenue minus cost of goods, shipping, platform fees)
- Is there recurring revenue (subscriptions, digital products) or only one-time sales?

#### B. Customer Health

- **Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC):** How much does it cost to acquire one new customer? Is it going up or down?
- **Customer Lifetime Value (LTV):** How much does the average customer spend over their entire relationship with Legendary Arena? LTV should be at least 3x CAC.
- **Retention rate:** What percentage of customers buy a second time?
- **Net Promoter Score (NPS):** Would customers recommend Legendary Arena to a friend? (Survey-based)
- **Review and feedback quality:** What are customers saying? Patterns in complaints = product or process gaps.

#### C. Operational Independence (The "Bus Factor")

The most important question for any business owner: **What happens if Jeff can't work for 30 days?**

- Can orders be fulfilled without Jeff? (Automation, third-party logistics, or documented manual process?)
- Can customer support questions be answered without Jeff? (FAQ, knowledge base, documented responses?)
- Can the website stay up without Jeff? (Monitoring, auto-restart, Cloudflare caching?)
- Can finances be tracked without Jeff? (Bookkeeping system, not spreadsheets in Jeff's head?)

Every "no" is a vulnerability. The Auditor identifies these and prioritizes making each one a "yes."

#### D. Brand Equity

- Does the brand have recognition beyond Jeff's personal network?
- Is there a community (Discord, Facebook group, YouTube subscribers) that would survive a change of ownership?
- Is the brand origin story (the game nights with Tex) known and resonant? Does it show up in customer reviews and word-of-mouth?
- Is the brand associated with quality, or just low price?

#### E. Scalability Assessment

- If demand doubled tomorrow, could you fulfill it? What breaks first? (Inventory? Shipping? Support?)
- Are processes documented enough to train a new employee or VA?
- Which tasks does Jeff do that could be automated or delegated?
- What's the next hire that would create the most leverage? (Likely: a part-time shipping/fulfillment person, or a content creator for YouTube)

#### F. Financial Controls

Bringing it full circle to the accounting parallel:
- Is there a clear separation between business and personal finances?
- Are all revenue sources tracked and reconcilable?
- Are all expenses categorized and reviewed monthly?
- Is there a profit and loss (P&L) statement generated monthly?
- Are taxes estimated and set aside quarterly?
- Is there a cash reserve (at least 3 months of operating expenses)?

### Deliverable
A **Quarterly Business Health Report** with scores:

| Category | Score (1–5) | Notes |
|----------|-------------|-------|
| Revenue health & diversification | | |
| Customer acquisition efficiency (CAC vs LTV) | | |
| Customer retention & satisfaction | | |
| Operational independence (bus factor) | | |
| Brand equity & community | | |
| Scalability readiness | | |
| Financial controls & transparency | | |
| Documentation & process maturity | | |
| **Overall business health** | | |

### The Auditor's Rules
1. **"Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity, cash is king."** Top-line revenue means nothing if margins are thin and cash is tight.
2. **"Assume Jeff is gone."** If the business can't survive 30 days without the founder, it's not a business — it's a job.
3. **"A buyer sees risk as cost."** Every undocumented process, every single point of failure, reduces the business's value.
4. **"Growth without margin is just expensive exercise."** No margin, no mission.

### How to Run This Agent
> "You are the Auditor agent for Legendary Arena. Evaluate the overall health of the business. Review: revenue trends, customer metrics (CAC, LTV, retention), operational independence (could the business run for 30 days without the founder?), brand equity, scalability, and financial controls. Score each category 1–5. Identify the top 3 vulnerabilities that would concern a potential buyer or investor."

## 7. The Owner's Role: The Orchestrator

### Jeff's Position in the System

The Orchestrator does NOT do the work of any single agent. The Orchestrator's job is to ensure the system runs, the agents communicate, and the business moves toward its vision.

**The Orchestrator's responsibilities:**

**Sets the vision and priorities**
- Decides which products to develop next
- Decides which markets to pursue
- Sets the quarterly goals that all four agents align to
- Makes the final go/no-go on campaigns, product launches, and major expenditures

**Allocates resources**
- Decides how much time and money each agent's work receives
- Balances investment between product development (code agents) and revenue generation (business agents)
- Decides when to do it yourself vs. hire/outsource vs. automate

**Resolves conflicts between agents**
- The Strategist wants to launch a new campaign, but the Analyst says the last one hasn't been measured yet → the Orchestrator decides
- The Creator wants to build content for a new product, but the Auditor says the current product line isn't profitable enough → the Orchestrator decides
- The Builder wants to add features, but the Evaluator says documentation is behind → the Orchestrator decides

**Reviews the dashboards**
- The Orchestrator's primary tool is the Legendary Arena dashboard
- Weekly: check the Analyst's campaign metrics
- Monthly: review the business scorecard
- Quarterly: review the Auditor's health report and the Evaluator's code readiness report

**The Orchestrator's Rules:**
1. **"Stay above the work."** The moment the Orchestrator starts writing code, creating content, or running analytics, one of the agents has no oversight. Delegate, even if the delegate is a Claude session.
2. **"Follow the dashboard."** Decisions based on data from the Analyst and Auditor, not gut feeling.
3. **"No margin, no mission."** Every decision filters through this lens: does this generate margin that funds the mission, or does it just feel productive?

## 8. The Sales Funnel — Checks at Every Stage

Each stage of the funnel has a responsible agent and a specific checkpoint. No customer moves through the funnel without each stage being owned and measured.

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                        SALES FUNNEL                             │
│                                                                 │
│  AWARENESS ──▶ INTEREST ──▶ CONSIDERATION ──▶ DECISION ──▶ RETENTION
│                                                                 │
│  Strategist    Creator      Creator          Analyst     Analyst │
│  owns the      owns the     owns the         owns the    owns   │
│  targeting     content      persuasion       conversion  the    │
│                                              tracking    follow │
│                                                          up     │
│  Analyst       Analyst      Analyst          Auditor     Auditor│
│  verifies      measures     measures         reviews     reviews│
│  reach         engagement   intent signals   checkout    LTV    │
│                                              process           │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Stage-by-Stage Ownership

**Stage 1: Awareness — "They know we exist"**
- Strategist defines where the target audience spends time (YouTube, Facebook groups, Reddit, Google search, comic conventions)
- Creator produces content for those channels (videos, posts, ads, SEO articles)
- Analyst measures: impressions, reach, website visits from each channel, cost per impression
- Checkpoint: Are we reaching the RIGHT people, or just a lot of people? (Strategist reviews audience quality)

**Stage 2: Interest — "They're paying attention"**
- Creator produces value-first content that builds trust before asking for a sale (build tutorials, behind-the-scenes videos, free guides, the origin story)
- Creator captures interest: email signup forms, YouTube subscribe CTA, free downloadable guide in exchange for email
- Analyst measures: email signups, time on site, pages per session, YouTube watch time, social follows
- Checkpoint: Is the content generating leads, or just views? (Analyst checks email list growth vs. traffic)

**Stage 3: Consideration — "They're thinking about buying"**
- Creator produces product-specific content: product demos, comparison guides ("Why our kit vs. buying parts separately"), customer testimonials, FAQ pages
- Creator builds the product pages and landing pages that convert visitors to buyers
- Analyst measures: product page views, add-to-cart rate, email click-through rate on product announcements
- Checkpoint: Are people looking at products, or just reading blog posts? (Analyst checks consideration-stage metrics)

**Stage 4: Decision — "They're buying (or not)"**
- Analyst owns the checkout experience: is it smooth? Where do people drop off? What's the cart abandonment rate?
- Creator builds abandoned cart email sequences (reminder, discount offer, urgency)
- Analyst measures: conversion rate (visitors → buyers), average order value, cart abandonment rate, checkout completion rate
- Checkpoint: If people add to cart but don't buy, why? (Analyst diagnoses: shipping cost? Trust? Complexity?)

**Stage 5: Retention — "They come back and tell friends"**
- Creator builds post-purchase experience: thank you email, assembly support emails, "share your build" invitation, request for review
- Analyst measures: repeat purchase rate, review submission rate, referral traffic, email engagement post-purchase
- Auditor reviews: customer lifetime value, Net Promoter Score, support ticket volume and themes
- Checkpoint: Are one-time buyers becoming repeat customers and advocates? (Auditor assesses long-term customer health)

## 9. Dashboard Integration

The Legendary Arena dashboard should give the Orchestrator (Jeff) a single screen that shows the health of both the code and the business.

### Dashboard Panels

**Top Row — Business Vitals (The Auditor's View)**
- Monthly revenue (trend line, last 12 months)
- Gross margin percentage
- Cash on hand
- Customer count (total and new this month)

**Second Row — Funnel Health (The Analyst's View)**
- Awareness: monthly website visitors (by channel)
- Interest: email list size and growth rate
- Consideration: product page conversion rate
- Decision: checkout completion rate and AOV
- Retention: repeat purchase rate and review count

**Third Row — Campaign Performance (The Creator & Strategist's View)**
- Active campaigns: status, spend-to-date, performance vs. KPI
- Top-performing content: which pieces drive the most traffic/sales
- A/B test results: which variants are winning

**Bottom Row — Alerts & Action Items**
- Funnel leaks detected (red flags from the Analyst)
- Campaigns underperforming KPIs (needs Strategist review)
- Overdue tasks from any agent
- Quarterly Auditor review due date

### Dashboard Rule
**"If it's not on the dashboard, it's not being managed."** Every KPI, every funnel metric, every campaign result must be visible in one place. The Orchestrator should never need to log into five different tools to understand the state of the business.

## 10. Checklists

### Strategist Checklist (Before Any Campaign Launches)
- [ ] Target audience (ICP) defined with demographics, psychographics, and where they spend time
- [ ] Core message defined as a transformation, not a feature list
- [ ] Channels selected with rationale (why this platform for this audience?)
- [ ] Budget allocated per channel
- [ ] Timeline set with milestones
- [ ] KPIs defined and measurable (specific numbers, not "increase awareness")
- [ ] Campaign brief written and saved to `/docs/campaigns/`

### Creator Checklist (Before Content Goes Live)
- [ ] Content matches the campaign brief's target audience and message
- [ ] CTA is clear and specific in every piece
- [ ] A/B variant created (if applicable)
- [ ] Brand voice and tone are consistent
- [ ] All links work and UTM parameters are appended
- [ ] Analyst has confirmed tracking is in place
- [ ] Content logged in the content tracker with funnel stage noted

### Analyst Checklist (Before and After Campaign)
- [ ] Analytics tracking confirmed (Google Analytics, Meta Pixel, email tracking)
- [ ] UTM parameters verified on all campaign links
- [ ] Conversion tracking tested end-to-end (click → purchase → confirmation)
- [ ] Baseline metrics captured before launch
- [ ] Weekly reports generated during active campaign
- [ ] Funnel leak analysis completed after campaign
- [ ] Results compared to campaign brief KPIs
- [ ] Recommendations documented: continue, adjust, or kill

### Auditor Checklist (Quarterly)
- [ ] Revenue trend reviewed (growing, flat, declining)
- [ ] Product-level profitability calculated
- [ ] CAC and LTV calculated and compared (LTV should be 3x+ CAC)
- [ ] Retention rate calculated
- [ ] Operational independence assessed (30-day test)
- [ ] All processes documented in writing
- [ ] Financial controls reviewed (separate accounts, monthly P&L, tax reserves)
- [ ] Brand equity and community health assessed
- [ ] Scalability bottlenecks identified
- [ ] Quarterly Health Report completed with scores

### Orchestrator Checklist (Weekly)
- [ ] Dashboard reviewed (all panels green?)
- [ ] Any agent blocked or behind? Resolve.
- [ ] Priorities aligned with current quarter's goals
- [ ] Budget on track? Overspend anywhere?
- [ ] One decision made that moves the business forward (no stagnation)

## 11. The Accounting Parallel

| Accounting Principle | Business Agent Equivalent |
|---------------------|---------------------------|
| Separation of duties | Different agents for strategy, creation, measurement, and oversight |
| No self-approval | The Creator doesn't measure their own results (Analyst does) |
| Budget authorization | Strategist sets the budget; Analyst confirms spend was effective |
| Reconciliation | Analyst compares actual results to planned KPIs (like reconciling books to bank statements) |
| Internal audit | Quarterly Auditor review of overall business health |
| External audit | Fresh AI session (or different platform) reviews the business with no prior context |
| Financial statements | Monthly P&L, dashboard metrics, quarterly health report |
| Audit trail | Campaign briefs, content logs, performance reports — all documented |
| Segregation of assets | Business finances separate from personal finances |
| Fiduciary duty | Orchestrator's obligation: every dollar spent must serve the mission |
| Annual report | Quarterly business health scorecard — the "balance sheet" of the company |
| Fraud detection | Analyst catches vanity metrics being used to justify bad spend (the business equivalent of embezzlement — spending company money with nothing to show for it) |

## 12. Transferable Principles

These principles work for Legendary Arena, but they work for ANY business — a bakery, a consulting firm, a nonprofit, a second online store. The roles are universal:

**Principle 1: Separate the planning from the doing from the measuring.**
The person who decides what to do (Strategist) should not be the same person who does it (Creator) or measures whether it worked (Analyst). When one person does all three, they unconsciously bias toward "it's working" because they're invested in the outcome.

**Principle 2: Every dollar gets a brief and a report.**
No money is spent without a written plan (campaign brief) and a written result (Analyst report). This is the financial audit trail of the business.

**Principle 3: The funnel is the business.**
Every business has a funnel: attract → engage → convert → retain. The stages might have different names, but the structure is universal. Own each stage separately.

**Principle 4: The dashboard is the cockpit.**
You can't fly a plane by looking out the window. You need instruments. The dashboard shows the instruments. If a metric isn't on the dashboard, you're flying blind on that metric.

**Principle 5: The founder must become the orchestrator.**
In the early days, the founder does everything. That's survival. But the business can't grow until the founder stops being every agent and starts orchestrating the agents. The system described in this document is the roadmap for that transition.

**Principle 6: No margin, no mission.**
Everything circles back to this. The Strategist targets the right customers. The Creator produces content that converts. The Analyst proves the math works. The Auditor confirms the business is healthy. The Orchestrator ensures margin exists to fund the mission. If any link breaks, the mission stalls.
